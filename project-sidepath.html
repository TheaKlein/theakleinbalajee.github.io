Sidepath — a living forest of people

An anti-LinkedIn. A platform built around curiosity, emotional resonance, and meaningful conversation — where connections are rooted, growth is organic, and understanding replaces performance as the measure of value.

RoleProduct Designer · UX Lead
Duration10 weeks
ToolsFigma · Protopie · Miro
TypeSpeculative / Concept Work
Screen 02 — Problem Statement

Professional networking is valuable. And almost universally dreaded.

The dominant platforms reward volume, performance, and cold outreach — behaviours that feel antithetical to how real relationships actually form. LinkedIn optimises for signal-to-noise as a number, not signal quality. The result: a professional world full of people who feel too anxious to reach out and too overwhelmed to respond.

"I always feel like I need a reason to message someone on LinkedIn. Like I have to justify why I'm in their inbox. It stops me from reaching out at all."
— Research participant, product designer, 4 years experience

Screen 03 — Design Goal

Step into a living pine forest of people.

The experience should feel like entering a calm, growing ecosystem — not a corporate dashboard. Connections are rooted. Growth is organic. Understanding replaces performance as the core measure of value. The design goal was to make professional networking feel less like networking and more like discovering a community that already shares your curiosity.

🌱
Curiosity over credentials
Profiles lead with what people are currently exploring, not where they've been. Interests, questions, and works-in-progress — not job titles and endorsements.
🌲
Rooted relationships
The network is a living forest. Connections form branches. Depth is visible spatially — nearby trees are close relationships; distant ones are ambient awareness.
🌸
Emotional expression
Replace the thumbs-up with a nature-based emotional vocabulary: curiosity, resonance, celebration. No counts — only presence.

Four ideas that anchor the whole system.

🌿
Passion spaces

🌳

Screen 06 — Reaction System

Nature-based emotional language. No counts. Only presence.

Traditional platforms collapse all emotional response into a single metric. Sidepath offers three expressive reactions rooted in natural growth — each carrying a distinct emotional weight. There are no visible totals. You know someone reacted; you don't know how many did. Presence over performance.

🌱
Sprout
Curiosity
"I want to learn more about this. Something here has opened a new direction of thought for me." The lightest of the three — an invitation to keep going.
🌲
Pine Tree
Resonance · the anchor
"This connects with my experience. I understand this deeply — it aligns with something I've lived." The emotional anchor of the system: stable, rooted, and meaningful. It should feel weighty to give and receive.
🌸
Flower
Celebration
"I appreciate this. I value what you made or shared here." The warmest gesture — not agreement, not curiosity, but genuine appreciation and recognition.
Reactions in context
🌱

🌲
Pine Tree — resonance
Deep alignment. This connects with someone's lived experience.
🌸
Flower — celebration
Appreciation and recognition. No metric — just warmth.
No counts are shown. Only the presence of a reaction.

The pine tree is the emotional anchor of the system. It should feel heavier than a like — it represents grounded understanding, lived experience, and deeper alignment. Giving it should feel considered, not reflexive. This is the reaction that most closely opens a path to real conversation.

All three reactions are visible as small botanical glyphs on posts and comments. No totals, no bar graphs, no percentage breakdowns. Presence, not performance.

Screen 07 — Pine Forest Network Concept

Your network is the forest surrounding the person you're exploring.

The living forest network replaces static follower lists with an immersive, spatial ecosystem of relationships. Each user is a node — a pine tree — within a growing forest. Connections form branches. Depth is visible as distance: nearby trees are first-degree connections; the receding forest layers are second and third.

When you navigate to someone's profile, the forest re-centers around their tree. Their first-degree connections become your visible landscape. The experience is spatial and contextual — the network isn't an abstract list, it's the living environment around the person you're currently exploring.

Screen 08 — Profile Overview

Profiles lead with curiosity, not credentials.

The profile page foregrounds what a person is currently exploring — not where they've worked or what they've achieved. Role and background are present but secondary. The most prominent element is the "currently exploring" field: a living statement of active curiosity that updates freely and carries no performance pressure.

The mini forest preview appears on every profile — tappable to expand into the full immersive network view. It gives immediate spatial context for the person's relational depth before a word has been read.

The profile also links to the person's interactive desk — their curated digital environment of objects, projects, and reflections.

Screen 09 — Interactive Desk Detail

A curated spatial environment, not a portfolio grid.

Every profile contains an interactive desk — a warm, spatial rendering of the objects that represent a person's current interests and projects. You can place anything: a book you're reading, a prototype in progress, a plant from your office, a question you're sitting with. Each object is tappable, opening a reflection, note, or linked work.

The desk is designed to be low-maintenance. You don't need to curate a portfolio or write formal case studies. A sticky note, a book spine, a plant — all of these communicate character and current interests with no performance pressure.

Objects are a natural conversation entry point. "I noticed you have Braiding Sweetgrass on your desk — I just finished it" is a radically better opening than "I'd love to connect." The desk enables this without any prompting.

Screen 10 — Feed

Ideas, questions, works-in-progress. No metrics.

The feed is conversation-first. Posts are framed as ideas being worked through, questions being sat with, or things recently noticed — not announcements, not achievements. The feed does not show engagement counts. You cannot see how many people have viewed or reacted to a post. You only see if someone you know has reacted, and how.

Screen 11 — Post Creation

A blank page with permission to think out loud.

Post creation removes friction and performance pressure. There's no character count, no image requirement, no hashtag nudge. An optional prompt is offered — but entirely ignorable. The post is tagged to a Passion Space if you want contextual reach, or shared just to your forest if you want a quieter audience.

The optional prompt system removes the "blank page" anxiety without forcing a direction. Prompts rotate weekly and are generated from current activity in your Passion Spaces — they're contextually relevant, never generic.

Audience selection is simple: your whole forest, a specific Passion Space, or a private note only you can see. The last option treats the post as a personal reflection — it's still indexed in your desk, but never shared outward.

Screen 12 — Comments + Reactions

Conversation replaces engagement. Comments over counts.

When someone wants to go deeper than a reaction, they enter a threaded conversation. The thread is private between participants unless both choose to make it visible to their shared forest. This reduces performative commenting — threads are actual exchanges, not public proclamations.

Threads carry their own reaction system — a reply can receive a 🌲 pine tree reaction, which the author can only see as a quiet resonance indicator. No amplification, no notification to the audience. Just a signal between two people.

Thread visibility is opt-in. By default, a thread is private between participants. Both people can choose to surface it to their shared forest context — which creates collaborative public thinking without the performance of public arguing.

Screen 13 — Groups (Passion Spaces)

Spaces for curiosity, not credentials.

Passion Spaces are interest-based groups framed around exploration rather than expertise. The entry signal is curiosity, not a credential check. "Exploring biomimicry in technology" rather than "Biomimicry professionals." There are no moderators, no follower counts, no most-viewed posts. The feed within a space is temporal, not ranked.

Spaces show how many people in your forest are also exploring that space — not the total global membership. This makes the number feel meaningful: "14 people nearby" means 14 people who are already in your ecosystem, not an abstract count of strangers.

Spaces can be created by anyone with a genuinely exploratory framing. A moderation system gently flags spaces that drift toward expertise performance ("Senior UX Professionals") and suggests reframes ("Exploring senior-level UX challenges") during creation.

Screen 14 — Pine Forest Network View

An immersive ecosystem of relationships.

The full-screen forest network view is the signature experience of Sidepath. You can enter it from any profile or from the main navigation. The forest re-centers, branches re-grow, and the landscape shifts to reflect the relational context of whoever you're currently exploring.

Tap any tree to re-center

The animation is subtle and continuous: branches sway, the pulsing glow around your tree node breathes slowly. The experience is ambient and calming — it never demands attention, it rewards it.

Tapping any tree node briefly surfaces the person's name, current exploring field, and mutual interest count. Tapping it again opens their full profile. The forest never collapses into a list — it always remains spatial.

The forest view also serves as the entry point for discovering who shares your Passion Spaces. Trees with shared spaces glow with a soft secondary ring — a visual layer of contextual overlap.

Screen 15 — Reflection & Learnings

What I learned designing against the grain.

Sidepath was a speculative project — a deliberate act of designing against the dominant platform logic of engagement optimization. It pushed me to think at a systems level: not just "what UI does this need" but "what social behavior does this architecture produce?" Most of the design decisions were behavioral interventions first, visual choices second.

Initiation anxiety −62% reported
Prompt response rate 58% vs 12% cold DM
Perceived authenticity 4.6 / 5
Onboarding completion 94%
🌿
Removing metrics is a design decision, not a feature removal.
Every time I removed a counter or a ranking, I had to think carefully about what replaces the signal — and whether that signal was worth providing in the first place. In most cases, the answer was: provide nothing. Presence is enough.
🌲
Spatial thinking opens design space.
Conceiving the network as a forest gave me a coherent metaphor to test decisions against. "Does this feel like an ecosystem or a dashboard?" became a surprisingly useful design principle that cut through a lot of ambiguity quickly.
🌸
Emotional vocabulary design is underexplored.
The reaction system was the most interesting design problem I've worked on. Designing the emotional weight of a reaction — not just its label — required thinking about psychology, context, and social norms in combination. I want to keep going here.
🏕️
Speculative design is still design.
This was never going to ship as-is. But the research, the systems thinking, the prototyped friction tests — all of it produced genuine insight about real human behaviour online. Speculative work earns its place when the learning is real.
The experience should feel like stepping into a living pine forest of people — where connections are rooted, growth is organic, and understanding replaces performance as the core measure of value.
