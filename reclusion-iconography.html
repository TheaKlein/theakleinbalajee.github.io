01
Overview

What is Reclusion?

Reclusion is a card advantage deckbuilder set across multiple historical and fantastical factions — spanning medieval Europe, ancient Rome, the forest wilds, and the China Sea. Players build decks, apply status effects to units, and outmanoeuvre opponents through layered buffs, debuffs, and card synergies.

Status effects are a core mechanical pillar of the game. They appear on card faces, battlefield tokens, and the game HUD throughout every match. As the UX designer responsible for the icon system, my scope covers the full design of a status effect iconography system — from initial silhouette explorations through to a Figma component library and handoff documentation.

Game context
Card advantage deckbuilder with multiple factions. Status effects applied and tracked via cards — icons must be instantly legible on card faces, battlefield tokens, and UI panels at the same time.
My scope
End-to-end icon system design. Shape language, colour system, size constraint testing, cross-cultural consideration, and Figma component documentation. Working in collaboration with the broader design team.
Effects in scope
Seven status effects for the initial system: Poisoned, Burning, Frozen, Stunned, Cursed, Weakened, and Hasted. Additional effects may be scoped after phase one is complete.
Core constraint
All icons must read clearly at 16×16px — the smallest display size used on battlefield tokens. Every design decision is tested at this size before development.
Where I am now
Early ideation and research phase. Establishing design principles, mapping the problem space, and exploring silhouette directions for each effect before any final shapes are committed to. This page is a living document — it will be updated as the work progresses.
02
The Problem

What makes this hard?

Status effect iconography in card games looks simple from the outside. It isn't. These are the core design tensions I'm working through before putting pencil to paper.

Process note: I'm mapping these problems explicitly before sketching anything — because the biggest failure mode in icon design is jumping to visual solutions before understanding the constraints. Every problem below will shape the evaluation criteria for each design direction.
1
How do you keep icons legible at 16×16px?
Card games are punishing for icon design. Intricate ornament and cultural detail look compelling at full size and collapse entirely at token size. Silhouette clarity is the foundational constraint — all exploration starts here, not at the finished render.
2
How do you distinguish similar-feeling effects from each other at speed?
Poisoned, Burning, Weakened, and Cursed all feel like "bad things happening to your unit" — but each has a distinct mechanical meaning. Players need to tell them apart mid-combat without stopping to read a tooltip. Shape differentiation has to do this work, not colour alone.
3
How do you make one icon system feel right across three cultural contexts?
The game spans Rome, forest, and China Sea factions — each with a distinct visual identity. Icons need to feel culturally resonant within each faction's card art without requiring players to learn a different visual language per faction. The system has to feel like one thing, not three.
4
How do you show buff vs debuff immediately — especially when only one effect is positive?
Six of the seven effects are debuffs. Hasted is the sole buff in this set. Players should never have to wonder if something good or bad just happened to their unit. Directionality, shape energy, and expansion vs contraction need to carry this distinction beyond colour.
5
How do you signal both effect type and stack severity in a single icon?
Players may need to know not just "Poisoned" but "Poisoned at level 3." The icon system needs a way to carry severity information — whether through the icon itself, a modifier system, or a separate but related visual — without cluttering the card layout.
03
Design Principles

How I'm approaching it

These principles were established before any sketching began — so that every design direction has something concrete to be evaluated against. They're working principles, not final rules, and will be tested and refined through the process.

Principle 01 — Foundation
Silhouette first. Always.
Every icon must be fully distinguishable as a flat black silhouette before any colour, texture, or detail is applied. Colour reinforces — it does not carry — meaning. This ensures the system works in greyscale, at 16px, and in print reproduction.
Principle 02 — Size
Design at 16px first.
All icons are sketched and tested at the smallest display size — the 16×16px battlefield token — before being scaled up. Detail is added only where it survives that constraint. Decorative complexity comes last, not first.
Principle 03 — Shape
Shape carries type.
Sharp, angular, and jagged = harm. Rounded and flowing = movement or speed. Broken or inverted = debilitation. Expanding or radiating = positive. This shape vocabulary is applied consistently across all seven effects so the system is learnable.
Principle 04 — Direction
Buff vs debuff must be immediate.
Upward-pointing and expanding forms signal positive effects. Downward, contracting, or broken forms signal negative effects. With only one buff in the set, this distinction needs to be unambiguous at a glance — directionality does this before colour is even considered.
Principle 05 — System
One system, three expressions.
One base silhouette per effect, with faction-flavoured surface treatments applied by the art team. Players don't relearn the system between factions — but the art team retains the ability to express cultural identity within each card's visual language.
Principle 06 — Construction
Grid discipline.
All icons share a 24×24 construction grid with consistent optical weight, padding zone, and stroke weight range. Icons should feel like a coherent family even when their individual forms are very different — a serpent coil and a snowflake should sit together naturally.

Icon System

Status effects — design intent

Seven effects in active exploration. Each entry documents the mechanical meaning, motif directions being considered, open design questions, and current stage. Final icons will replace these placeholders as explorations are resolved.

Shape language
Sharp & angular = harm. Rounded & flowing = movement. Broken or inverted = debilitation. Expanding or radiating = buff.
Colour — secondary only
Each effect has a signature hue but colour never carries meaning alone. Greyscale silhouette must be fully legible and distinguishable first.
Construction grid
24×24 grid, consistent optical weight, same padding zone across all seven. The set should feel like a family at a glance.
